Meteora – Hanging Monastries

 

What are Meteora?

Meteora or “suspended in the air” is the largest rock formation in the world. The highest one reach over 600 meters above sea level. Rocks are mainly maid of sandstone and agglomerate. Name Meteora comes from Greek word “meteoros” and means “raised up” or “high in the air”. Name perfectly reflects landscape as from distance the rocks looks like they would hang in the air. Meteora is located few kilometers northwest of the town of Kalabaka, which in wider scale is located north-west of the plains of Thessaly.

 

History of Meteora

According to many historical records, the meteors uplifted due to erosion and movement of the earth. Many rivers flowed into Lake Thessaly, carrying sediment and stones. They have accumulated over the years. After the water retreated due to the earthquake, numerous cracks and scratches appeared in the monolith hardened from the sediments that the lake had built for thousands of years. Over time, the rocks began to delaminate, erosion processes took place and eventually formed separated boulders and rock chimneys called Meteors.

Uniqueness of this terrain relativity quickly start attracting all different type of mystics, hermits and, finally, monks, who saw in these unusual shapes the work of the god himself.

Word “monk” come from Greek “monachos”. Monos in Greek mean – solo, one, alone, single.

Etymology best explains of the hermit and monastic movement, where men was getting close to his creator by prayer and asceticism, ideally somewhere in isolation. No wonder that Meteora always drawn to itself this type of people.

Standing on top of those peaks you can feel like you are suspended between earth and sky.

First hermits arrived to this place in 11th century, but to beginning they habituated caves and caverns. They would stay couple of days at the time praying and meditate in total silence. In 12th century new religious community is getting formed, and habitats small temple at the bottom of rocks. In 14th century when Turks started to invade Greece, monks decidet to move to a safer place which was top of Meteora.

 

Monasteries

First Monastery was built in 14th century. In 1356-1372 Holy Monastery of Great Meteoron was build. In oncoming decades next Monasteries was build. Each of them was located at difficult to reach rock. The only option to get to it was to climb rock wall, later stage they upgraded to thrown down ladders and ropes. The first stairs were built in 1920, when new route in rock was expressed.

Recent decades brought more truism to place; hence all active Monasteries had built access stairs to them.

In 1988 Meteora Monasteries was included in the UNESCO World Heritage List and the Meteora-Antichassia area is part of the Natura 2000 network hosting some rare species of birds and flowers.

At the peak of its prosperity, 24 Orthodox monasteries operated here. Only 6 are now active and are open to visitors. The others are either in ruins or uninhabited. If we look around carefully during the walk, we should be able to spot some of the ruins, mainly fragments of the walls. The monasteries, especially during the Turkish occupation, were places where Greek traditions were cultivated, and important treasures of Greek culture were hidden. Some of the collections (including books, paintings, letters) are on display in special rooms in the monasteries. Many of the collections were moved during World War II. The monasteries were usually male, although unlike the monasteries on Athos, there were also female monasteries to which only women were allowed.

Holly Monestery of Great Meteoron

The most impressive, most complexed and the most visited at the same time. The Great Meteor Monastery (also known as the Monastery of the Transfiguration or the Great Meteoron and the Great Meteora) was founded in 1340 by St. Athanasius, who, according to legend, was carried up the mountain by an eagle. Inside, on several levels, we will see, a tower with a lift, large two-level observation decks, an ossuary of skulls, a museum of folklore, and an old book collections (some even from the 9th and 11th centuries). There is also the monastery kitchen. The monastery chapel is impressive and the biggest one of all. The entrance to the monastery leads through a tunnel and stairs carved in the rock. The size of the monastery area resembles a small castle.

 

Holly Monastery of Varlaam

It is a second most visited Monatery of them all. It owns name to the hermit – anchorite Varlaam, who first inhabited the rock in 14th century. Monastery was build from three churches and wooden cistern for rain water as there was no running water there. Currently visitors can admire monuments from 16th and 17th centuries, which include the main Temple of All Saints with rich iconostasis, Three Hierarch Chapel, former hospice/hospital. There is also Saints Cosmas and Damian Chapel with old refectory. Also beautifully preserved hoist tower with a winch and a net.

 

Holly Monastery of Rousanou

The Rousanou Monastery also known as St. Barbara Monastery was built in 1545 by two brothers from Giannena. Originally monastery was a place of refuge for prosecuted individuals and families during various historical changeability for nation. After restoration in 1980 monastery become nunnery, one of two operating ones. The road to the building leads through a bridge and a long staircase, where there is a free observation deck located with a wonderful view. Insite there is small museum with icons we can visit and two rooms Chapel. Outside there is another observation deck with benches, so tourist can sit down and admire stunning views this location has to offer.

 

Holly Monestery of St. Nicholas Anapafsas

Saint Nicholas Anapafsas Monastery was founded in 14th century, renovated in 16th century by Larissa Metropolitan St. Dionysius the Merciful and the Exarch Stagon monk Nikanor. In 1527 Monastery was painted by famous Cretan painter Theophanes Strelitzas. Over the years monastery was abandoned and decay, until in 1960 was restored and renovated by Archaeological Service. Its is the smallest and lowest level build monastery as needed to be adapted to the capabilities of an exceedingly small area on the rock. Inside consist of small St. Anthony Chapel and the crypt, where previously codes and heirlooms were stored. On next level there is another small room, which was a small church, and the next level contain old bank of the monastery, which after restoration was converted to an official residence.

 

Holly Monestery of St. Stephen

St. Stephen Monastery, was founded in 14th century and completed in the 15th and 16th century. Since 1961 it is habituated by sisterhood of nuns that lives there , and are known of their remarkable social actions. Monastery is located on the southern edge of the cluster of Meteora. Access to the monastery it is very easy via small stone bridge. Inside we will find beautiful courtyard with stunning gardens. Inside building there is also a religious museum where we can admire cross, icons, books and manuscripts. The collection also includes letter to the Patriarch of Constantinople from 1605 and old dictionaries dated 1499.

 

Holly Monestery of Holy Trinity

Holy Trinity Monastery was founded in 1488 by monk name Dometios. Beautiful frescos was made by painter and priest Antonios and his brother Nicholas in 1741 year. The most valuable monuments of the monastery were destroyed or robbed during the German occupation during World War II. Monastery owned 124 manuscripts which since 1953 are kept in St. Stephen Monastery. To the left of the entrance of Monastery there is located St. John the Baptist Chapel, a small rotunda carved into the rock. And at the end of the hallway there is catholicon of the monastery. It is a small two-columned church with a dome. Overall, it is most photographed Monastery and popularity gained after 1981 year, when James Bond movie was relisted “For your eyes only”. Behind production of this movie there is an interesting story, as Greek Bishop ( for the appropriate fee ) allowed filming crew to film some scenes inside of monastery without consulting with monks. Although they won dispute with state power for the rights to use the land, some of Monks moved out to monastery in Athos.

 

Tips before visiting Monasteries

  • In monasteries appropriate clothing and covering of shoulders and knees are required. Women, even if they are wearing trousers, are asked to gird their belts with a special scarf available at the entrance.
  • In many places inside is forbidden to take photographs, there is exceptions of courtyards and rooms which are prepared for tourists.
  • Be quite and respectful as monks and nuns still resident in Monasteries
  • Be prepared, if you need to use a toilet that it will be in the form of a hole in the ground.
  • There are shops in most monasteries. Where you can buy various devotional items (crosses, icons, rosary), typical tourist souvenirs (magnets, postcards or figurines) and food products made on the spot (jams, wine).
  • Monasteries also are homes for loads of stray and kind of homeless animals like dogs and cats, so be prepared to have few extra treats in your pocket.
 

When and how much time is needed to visit.

Most busy time of year in Meteora is between June and August, but you need to take on board it is the hottest time of year as well. Best months for visiting are April/May and September/October were temperatures usually flatulate between 15 to 22 degrees. It is worth avoiding Greek holidays and weekends, then, in addition to foreign tourists, there are also crowds of locals.

Distances between Monasteries and significant, so physical condition is also needed.

Most optimal time to visit all monasteries is between 2-4 day bearing in mind Meteora is not only Monasteries but beautiful nature around and some interesting places in Kalambaka and Kastraki.

Like The Byzantine Church of Kalambaka. Church is open every day from 8.30 till 13.00 and 15.00 till 20.00. There is a small entrance fee of €2.00.

Close to station you will also find a Meteora Natural History and Mushroom Museum. Museum is open Sunday – Friday 10.00 – 18.00 and Saturdays 10.00 – 19.00. Contact details are

Tours and walks

  • Walking – if you prepared for huge amount of activity, you could walk around all 6 Monasteries. This way of course will take much longer to visit all of them, and it will be very tiring, but views will compromise this inconvenience. You also need to be particularly careful when going up and down the rocks, as very uneven surfaces in places. There are no walking paths, you will be walking on the road, so extra carefulness is needed as sharing this space with vast number of cars on the road. Roads are very winding, can be fogy so wear reflective clothing, especially when dawn is coming. Some of places worth to look out for when walking around:
    • Walking from Kastraki towards St. Nicholas Anapafsas Monastery will be passing one of the caves, which people with climbing skill particularly adore. They climb inside and lave their country flags.
    • A little bit behind St. Nicholas Anapafsas Monastery there is walk way towards Varlaam Monastery, route is very complicated, as very uneven stone steps, but on the way we can find few caves worth looking at.
    • Route from Varlaam Monastery to Holy Trinity Monastery is probably the longest – nearly 4 kilometres. The road is mostly straight, although there are some more difficult parts. The walk is compensated by incredible charming views.
    Few extra tips for preparing for walking activities:
    • Make sure we have appropriate footwear for the journey, clothes and headgear
    • Have plenty of drinking water
    • In hotter days make regular breaks
    • Try to start your journey before sunrise, this way you will be able to see sunrise over the rocks
    • On route to and around Meteora there are no shops or cafes, so make sure you do take food with you.
  • You can travel around in a bus. In summer season buses from Kalambaka and Kastraki travels 4 times a day, but last one from Meteora is quite early so you can’t count to see sunset over horizon. Need to be aware that times and routes change very often so best way is to check before travel on KTEL Trikala website www.ktel-trikala.gr Tickets also can be bought with the driver.
  • If we have opportunity to rent a car, it will give us more comfort and flexibility. Even if we plan to walk all day, we can leave car in car park and come back at the end of the day. Every Monastery have few parking spaces, which might be occupied, but every viewpoint has bigger car park – which mean we would need to walk bit more to get to Monastery.
  • We can also buy guided tour, there are plenty of those available, from Athens, from Thessaloniki or from local bus stop in Kalambaka. That kind of activity will have limited time and we might not see all 6 Monasteries but will allowed also to local area and its perfect for someone who does not have ability to rent a car.

How to get to Meteora

The meteors are located within the Thessaly region, northern part of Greece. Access from large and popular cities such as Athens or Thessaloniki is not complicated. However, need to remember that using public transport from bigger cities will get us to station in Kalambaka. From there to Monasteries, you need to either take local bus, taxi, by trip or by foot. We can get to Kalambáka by using the train or the KTEL Trikala bus.


Train timetables and prices can be check at their official website https://tickets.trainose.gr/dromologia/#view=search


Buses timetables and prices can be check on KTEL Trikala website https://www.ktel-trikala.gr


All information much needed to plan trip to this most amazing place are also available on their official website https://meteora.com/
